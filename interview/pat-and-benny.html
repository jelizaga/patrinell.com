Pat & Benny Wright

ANDREW: What's the story with the Black Panthers living across the street?

BENNY: They used to live across the street in the second house over there, the guys who grew up to be the Panthers. Elmer Dixon I think was the one who started it in Seattle.

PAT: I don't know.

BENNY: Yeah. Elmer started it in Seattle.

PAT: I was scared of them.

BENNY: They used to march here in the park every day.

PAT: I know. That's why I said I was scared of them.

ANDREW: You were scared of them?

BENNY: They would march with their rifles on their shoulders.

PAT: They sure did. As my friend Mrs. Starkmeier would say, those were perilous times. Those were perilous times.

ANDREW: So you didn't stop and say "Hi?"

BENNY: Oh, I knew them. I knew the guys.

PAT: I didn't stop and say nothin’! I was afraid to get back into the house.

BENNY: Oh, I knew them very well.

PAT: They were very kind though. They did a lot of good things. They had the breakfast program and fed so many children. Nobody talks about the good they did. He's still around. They are still around.

BENNY: Their mother still lives over there.

PAT: She still lives over there. Second house on the left.

TIA: How old is she now?

PAT: She's got to be 90. She still does everything for herself. She's the person that needs to be interviewed. She has great stories to tell.

ANDREW: What is her name?

PAT: Dixon. Mrs. Dixon.

BENNY: I don't know her first name. I've just known her forever.

PAT: I just know that she was their mother, and she just stayed to herself. She actually kept them from doing a lot of detrimental things they would have done if it had not been for her.

BENNY: Really nice, though.

PAT: Really nice woman.

TIA: Please state your name and introduce yourselves.

BENNY: My name is Benny Wright and this is PAT: Wright, my wife.

PAT: My name is Patrinell.

BENNY: Patrinell Wright. No one else remembers it except for me and you and Greg.

TIA: And how long have you been married?

BENNY: Geez, 52 in June.

TIA: When where and how did you first meet?

PAT: At True Vine Baptist Missionary Church on the corner of 17th and East Fir.

BENNY: Yeah, we did. My sister came home and said there is this lady—girl—up there at the church: “She's pretty! She can sing!”

Me and my brother-in-law (Ozzie) was going to go out that night, so let's go and see what Brenda is talking about.

PAT: I ought to kill her, ha ha!

BENNY: Yeah. Pat walked by and I stuck my leg out so she could stumble over me. It gave me a chance to get a better look.

TIA: How did you feel about that when you stumbled over his leg?

PAT: I can't repeat the words I said inside.

BENNY: No, she just kept going to the piano. She didn't even say a word.

TIA: So how did you ask her out?

BENNY: Let's see, I saw her talking to a couple members of the church choir, Henry Jenkins. And I thought his conversation was kind of sketchy. I could do better than that in my sleep! And so I started talking to her too, to get him out of the way.

TIA (TO PAT):What did you think of Benny?

PAT: Not much.

TIA: Oh?

PAT: No, because I'm from the South and I'm used to men being a little bit more gentlemanly. Because you're taught as a child how you are supposed to treat a woman and so I didn't like the guys up here very much at all.

TIA: But something must've gotten your attention?

BENNY: Because I'm gentlemanly.

PAT: I won't go so far as to say that.

BENNY: Well “Northwest gentlemanly.”

PAT: Yeah that's right, ha ha!

TIA: So then how did you do the proposal?

BENNY: I don't even remember.

PAT: Well, he took me out and we were in the car from where we had gone. All of a sudden this box comes out and it has the ring in it, and he said, "Well, do you want to get married?"

And I said, "Why the hell not?"

So we did. This was six months later though.

BENNY: Didn't take long.

TIA: How many children have you raised, and what are their names?

PAT: We've actually raised a lot of children but there are two that lived here at the house, Julie Washington (Dr. Julie Washington) and PAT:rick Wright. We adopted him when he was six weeks. He was a newborn.

BENNY: The third person we raised here was our grandson.

PAT: He was our son’s son, Deshay.

TIA: Benny, what do you remember about Pastor Pat's early singing career?

BENNY: She could sing! She could sing, and sing well. The church that I belonged to— that we belonged to—True Vine, we thought we had the best little choir in Seattle.

PAT: And they did.

BENNY: And we used to always compete with other churches. I never could sing, but they would compete, church to church would compete. And when PAT: came along...that was our second pianist, you at Edna? It was still Sunday competitions but it really quickly got to be known that little True Vine had the best choir in the whole area. It made everybody really compete then. They really always wanted to always outdo True Vine. But there was not much chance of that happening. Later on of course another competition started.

We would go to different churches every Sunday. Somebody was always having a choir competition. They wanted to get you over there so they could do better, they thought. And a lot of them had good singers but they didn't have the people to put the music together.

PAT: I was trained in high school by Mr. Earl LaRue. Mr. Earl LaRue was the choir director at Turner High School which was an all-black school in Carthage, Texas. And he spotted the gifts that God has given me early on. He decided that he was going to work with me. But he told me, “You really don't need much work, because everything I can teach you, you already know how to do.”

Because I was raised in a Christian home, we were just expected as the pastor’s children to excel. And my sisters and brothers were very, very good at excelling. My brother, who is standing here right now, is one of those people who push anyway. He and I were born with respiratory diseases that they could never find reasons for, so I decided that I was going to overcome it and not let that be a handicap to me. I thank God that we did that.

TIA: Benny, do you recall when Pastor Pat recorded the single titled "I Let A Good Man Go?”

PAT: And "Little Love Affair" was on the flip side.

BENNY: That's the one I remembered.

TIA: What did you think of those?

BENNY: I've never been a person who would deal with music. I couldn't sing, and didn't try. But it was good because, like I said, she could sing! Looking back on it, I'm not sure I cared much about that song, but it came out pretty good. The guys who were backing her up were people I knew in the area. I was surprised they did as well as they did because I didn't know they could sing.

TIA: Who were they?

PAT: Just some guys from the neighborhood.

BENNY: One of them lived across the street from me, a couple of them.

PAT: There was a tremendous amount of talent and the Central District at that time.

BENNY: And I think a lot of that is because everybody went to a church.

PAT: Yeah, they were all from somebody's church.

BENNY: And I remember when I joined True Vine, Sister Reed just took me by the ear and put me in the choir. I couldn't sing, but it didn't matter. You got a chance to practice singing. Those who could of course practiced, not knowing they were going to be singers later. Some of them turned out well.

I was looking online the other day and I saw a friend of mine that I knew from way back in the ‘60s or late ‘50s even, Luther Rabb. I didn't know he could do all that. But Luther sang with War. He sang with Santana. Where was I when Luther was doing all this?

PAT: In church.

BENNY: Luther—his dad was a pastor, so I think he was probably in church, maybe. I just didn't know he could do that. And of course you know Jimi Hendrix was around here. Who else was out there who came in that group? We had several guys around here who were very good in music. We just didn't know they were good because we knew them every day.

Just like everybody is always talking about Jimi Hendrix. Jimi Hendrix to us was a bum, until he left and went to Frisco and got a chance to show everybody what he could do. One of my friends is living in Brazil now. Believe me, that was the biggest shock. His brother told me, "Yeah he's got a club down there and a musical studio.”

And I said, "When is Leo coming home?" And he said, "He's not coming home."

He's making his living there. He's married. He's got his music. Wasn't a great singer, but you know he would always jump up there.

I just said, "Wow. The people we've known!"

There have been quite a few come through Seattle.

I'd have breakfast every morning at this little restaurant down on Rainier. About half the guys in there I knew back in high school. And then the music Hall of Fame in Seattle— Griffin, drummer. Dave Holden, piano player. The Holden family is like the jazz history of Seattle. And I just knew them from high school. I never thought of them as being as good as they turned out to be, or probably always was. Yeah, it was different.

PAT: Yeah, I sang for Jimi Hendrix's funeral. It was so packed. People from all over the world were there.

TIA: How did you feel about being a gospel singer coming into this church with all these Europeans?

PAT: Didn't matter to me. My father was half-white, so I was used to having white blood around me all the time. It didn't bother me at all. They didn't ask me at all about color. All they were interested in me was, sing! And that's what I did.

BENNY: When you said church with all the Europeans what are you talking about? The Beatles, people like that?

PAT: Well actually, they were nicer to me than my own people.

BENNY: All those guys who knew Jimi Hendrix were the ones who showed up.

PAT: That is because he was accepted by the white community. There was a time in Seattle where if you sang blues or jazz you were totally ostracized. I was one of those people who didn't care about your ostracizing and put-downs. I was going to do what I could do. It didn't matter to me what genre it was as long as the lyrics were clean.

TIA: Can you talk about the time when you did sing in the night clubs in the area?

PAT: There used to be a lot of black clubs in Seattle and there aren't any now. But there used to be quite a bit, because the Central Area used to be highly populated with nothing but blacks because this was the only place the real estate agents, black or white, would show you a place to live. Seattle was a very prejudiced place then and it still is, but not nearly as bad as it used to be. And so all the clubs were right off of Jackson between Jackson Street and Fir. We were all boxed into this one little area. It was loaded with clubs.When I started to sing secular music for a while I was really ostracized by the churches: “You sinner! You're going to Hell!”

Well, Hell pays me, so shut up! That's been my attitude all my life! As long as I wasn't doing anything illegal—and I wasn’t going to do that—I was going to do what the talent allowed me to do.

BENNY: But all those guys who played music were raised in church too. When I moved to the Central Area, everybody was at church. They went to church regularly. They didn't particiPAT:e in the church operation much, but their parents made sure they went to church. Rather than fight at home they went to church.

PAT: Yeah, you didn't want to fight with mom and dad. You just did with you were told. That was an era where we may not have approved or enjoyed what our parents told us to do, but we did it anyway out of respect, which is something we've lost.

BENNY: I know as I grew up out of eight kids in the family, I was the only one that had to go with my mother. We lived in White Center. We only had one bus and it ran twice a day. She'd catch that bus and we come over here. I think I had been to every church in the Central Area before I ever moved here. I mean I'd go and go to sleep but... My mother, she was one of those kind of people who if the preacher was preaching... I think I got a whipping every Sunday. She always sat in the front row right in front of the preacher. And he had to stop preaching until she got done. And I didn't know what I had done wrong. But I was only, you know, 6 or 7 years old, always asleep or trying to play with somebody behind me or whatever. And boy, the next thing I know she'd... and next Sunday would be right back. I had been to Peoples [Institutional Baptist Church] before I had been to any other church. Peoples, Tabernacle, Goodwill.

PAT: Those churches are still in existence, to this day. I wasn't reared up here. I was reared in Carthage, Texas, actually in the country in a little area called Walnut Grove. We all were members of the Walnut Grove Baptist Church. And my father was the pastor of a little church called Pleasant Valley Baptist Church, and new Jerusalem Baptist Church in Fairplay, Texas which is 9 miles from Carthage. And when they discovered that I could play the piano and sing I began following my father. I'd play for his churches and I'd play for Walnut Grove. Nobody ever taught me how to play. It was just a gift from God. We had the baddest choir— "baddest" meaning the best choir around—under our tutelage.

BENNY: You know, when I first went down there, that made me laugh, because I couldn't understand how could you have a Baptist church here and a Methodist church about 50 feet away, maybe—I’ll say 50 yards. And the Methodists would sing with the kids in the Baptist Church twice a month. And I guess you guys went over to their church and sang twice a month. I said, “Why don't they just tear one of them down and come together as one church?”

PAT: No, those were denominations. Methodists and Baptists. I'm a Baptist. The church around the corner from us was at Methodist church. So we made sure we didn't have church on the same Sundays. We had church on the second and fourth and they had church on the first and third. On the fifth Sunday we all came together. But that is the kind of togetherness we had in the South that has never been prevalent up here. Yet the North is supposed to be more "progressive."

BENNY: We went to everybody's churches. That is about as progressive as we were. We went to everybody's churches, listened to everybody's preacher. Don't know what he said. But, you know, I knew all the preachers. A lot of them I grew up with, not the same age-wise, but grew up with as far as them chastising you for doing wrong. Reverend Mitchell of Goodwill—we lived across the street from him. Lived down the street from New Hope. Those were all friends of the family. You were expected to be at church. They would chastise you like you were their own children.

PAT: But when I say “togetherness” I mean, Walnut Grove would have church on the second and fourth Sunday. The Methodist Church would have church on the first and third Sunday. So we still went to each other's church. The congregation would go to the Methodist Church on their Sunday and the Methodists would come to our church on our Sunday so we got along very well. This is in the country. This is in Carthage, Texas in a place called Walnut Grove.

BENNY: Now, you guys had circuit preachers? That's why they only came twice a month?

PAT: Yes, they were circuit preachers who came twice a month.

TIA: Was your father a circuit preacher?

PAT: No, my father was full-time in Carthage, when he finally brought both congregations together at Pleasant Valley Baptist Church. He had two circuit churches: Pleasant Valley was at one point, and New Jerusalem at Fairplay was at one point. He would go there for two Sundays and then to Pleasant Valley. Then we became “citified”: everybody started coming to one church and that was a full-time church.

ANDREW: Getting back to the Central District, did you face discrimination when you were out looking for a house?

PAT: No, because they would only show you the black area.

BENNY: The guy who sold me the house, he used to own the property right back here.

PAT: Mr. Proctor.BENNY: When he first told me to come up here and talk to the lady, she didn't want to move. Madrona, when I grew up in high school, this was all Jewish. All of Madrona was Jewish. There was nobody black north of Martin Luther King.

PAT: Which was then called Empire Way.

BENNY: Then in ’68 they had a little riots in front of Garfield. One of my friends started that too.

PAT: It worked.

BENNY: But I came up here to talk to the lady and she said she didn't want to move. She started crying.

So I just went and told Proctor, “Look, she don't want to sell that house.” A couple days later he came back and said, “Go and talk to her now.” Somebody came and threw a garbage can through that front window, in order to make her a motivated seller. And so that's how we got the house. I'm sure that's what scared her out. And so we came up here. And there was Reverend [Samuel] McKinney next door. About three blacks on this street, three families and they were all black.

PAT: Reverend McKinney, he was like the leader of the African-American community. He pastored the largest black church in the city at that time, which was Mount Zion Baptist Church on the corner of 19th and Pine which is still in business. He is now fully retired. But he also led the Civil Rights Movement—not just the marches and that kind of thing —but to make this a better place in Washington State for African-Americans. I have to give him his due. He is in his early 90s now. He just lived in the house next-door.

BENNY: Thirty years I think we were probably neighbors. But he was one of the first ministers I met when I moved into the Central Area. He came in ’58, I believe. And I moved into the Central Area in ’57. I went to church and went to visit Mount Zion, of course. And when I graduated in ’61, he was at my graduation, because his assistant pastor, his son, was in the class with me. So we got to know each other really early anyway. For a very long time it seems. Then I went on to teaching his youngest daughter in school. So we've always known each other very well.

ANDREW: Back to the Central District: PAT: has always mentioned that blacks are moving out of not just the Central District but Seattle in general.

BENNY: Well, the central part of Seattle anyway. One, you can't afford to be here. I know just about everybody who has moved out to Kent or Renton. At one time the Central Area was just from I 90 to Madison. We were all in that little box. And so that was the place where we could move. You couldn't move to Beacon Hill. My best friend out of grade school, he was the first black family on Beacon Hill, because his dad was a real estate agent, Chuck Winston. There was nobody black up in there.

PAT: You couldn't tell that he was black anyway.

BENNY: But yeah, he was the first one up there. You didn’t get much past I 90 in terms of African-Americans living in, getting houses. There weren't many, period, in the Central Area. Like right now, there's about 3% in Washington. So you figure out of 7 million, that's about 200,000. And when I came to the Central Area it was about 70% percent African-American in the Central Area. Now it's about 10%. So there has been a big demographic change. And I've been around to see it all. Yeah, it's much, much different.

And then two, if you're going to build a house in the Central Area—you seen these big boxes they built? I know they are probably pretty nice inside, but that is about the only thing you can get a permit for to build in the Central Area. You better have the ducats to do it when you think about it. I had friends in construction too, and they are not going to get any work up here anymore. It's a big change. I've seen a big change.

When I first moved into the Central Area, Garfield was about 1/3rd black, 1/3rd Jewish, and 1/3 Asian. That's the way that was set up then. That's where they lived. That was new for me too. I didn't know anything about demographics then in Seattle. But it is a big, big change. Big change.

I asked these guys on the street, what made them move into Madrona? Because that was really driving the prices of these houses up. And the guy across the street said that he needed to be near the bridges, because he works in Bellevue but he rides his bike over there. I don't know why you would do that, but that is him—he is really into athletics. But he said he is close. If 520 breaks down, he can get on I90 and always get to work. A lot of guys here are into politics and into high tech, so they work over in Redmond and Kirkland and Bellevue. I think the only lady who works in the area lives across the street there. She is the new athletic director at the University of Washington. This was always a pretty well-off district in Seattle, even back in the old days. It was always very well-off—people with businesses. It was just a different place. We didn't come up here too often from down by 21st.

I remember coming up here to play basketball over at the park over here. We used to cut across this lawn, and the lady who owned the house would come out and say, “Could you please stay on the sidewalk?”

And then I remember one day PAT: was out there and some kids were going across the grass and she said, "Could you guys get on the sidewalk?"

I thought about that and said, "Boy, what goes around comes around! ”We weren't thinking about messing with the lady's yard—we were just taking the quickest way to the park. But yeah, it has been a big change and I think it has been a difficult change for a lot of people.

TIA: Can you talk about how you feel about those changes when you go out every day and come home?

BENNY: Most of them [neighbors] speak to me now. At first they didn't speak to me at all. If I passed them when I was out walking, their kids would always speak, but they would look off into the street, anywhere to keep from having eye contact. I would just walk on by and let them be themselves. And now they pretty much stop and want to have a conversation and at least they say "Hello."

And then what surprised me was that there were a number of ex-students of mine who live down over across 34th. I didn't know they were even up here. They were in class with my son, but they got up and moved up here too. And I'm sure it's only because they could afford to be here. Some of them were in some really well-placed jobs. But yeah it's just been different, you know?

But it wasn't as difficult for me, because I have always lived in a non-black community until I moved to the Central Area. We lived in White Center and there were, geez, 5 black families who lived up there that I knew of. When I went to Cleveland High School, there were 8 blacks in my senior class, so it's not like I had a lot of black students around me. Graduated and went to college. They were just the athletes there then. So it's been different, but you learn to put up with most anything.

TIA: Pastor Pat, how do you feel about it?

PAT: About the Central Area of Seattle? I hate it. I don't like it at all.

BENNY: When are we going to move?

PAT: I've been trying to get you to move back to Texas! I don't like it because we've lost our sense of closeness, our sense of protection. Everybody knew everybody. Everybody had everybody's back. That's gone. And I don't like it at all.

BENNY: There were some good things about segregation that, like she was just saying, we used to have a lot a little stores around here because we were forced to deal with each other.

PAT: I won't use the word “forced.”

BENNY: Well, that is because we would even go to the white stores, the little Brenner Brothers Bakeries. They were big around here. Then Stearn’s Markets. They had all the stores around here. But you always had a black stores too. When I came to the Central Area, that was new to me.

And what I started teaching up at Meany [Middle School], the first thing I recognized (which was just about a year later) was that the only people who were losing anything in Integration were blacks. We didn't have anything anymore. We were integrating into the white society.

You could watch the kids change. The kids in school, most of them didn't know anything different. But I taught history so I could talk to them about everything, race or whatever. That was the first thing I noticed. I got married and had gone down south and I could see how blacks lived with each other. And then I got up here and could recognize the fact that we weren't doing that anymore.

When I was in school we would live at the Rotary Boys Club. That's where we played ball and stuff. It was only blacks there. Then later on there was a Rotary Girls and Boys Club, and fewer and fewer blacks were in Rotary.

We had a really good cluster of teachers, so I can give them credit too. But we had almost no blacks at Meany in my classes. Blacks were in the other clusters, but in the cluster I was in (and we had 150 kids every day) I think I had 15 black kids. Where were they going? They were down the hall in the other clusters. Like I said, we had a really good cluster of teachers and the parents recognized that. And the next thing you know all my kids just about came from Magnolia Bluff and Laurelhurst. Almost all of them. One child in my class—Reverend McKinney's wife told them to take this girl out of my class so her friend’s daughter could get in. And this girl was a black student. So this girl came to me and said, “Mr. Wright, I don't want to leave.”

And I said, “So why are you leaving?”

She said the counselor told her she had to go to another cluster. I said, “So why is that?”

The little girl didn't know, so I went and asked the counselor and the counselor started crying. Miss Adams, she started crying: “Well they told me to take somebody out so that Rhoda could get in.”

I told her, “Look, that little girl, Christine, she can stay in my class as long as she is eligible to be there.”

So I told her [Christine], “You can sit at the table next to me. You don't have to go anywhere.”

That’s the way things were then. If you have the clout, you have the clout. And the school board is the one who sent the word down. Because they were afraid that Reverend McKinney would tell the black population, don’t vote for the levies. The Central Area always passed the levies to raise money for the school district. North End, Ballard never passed. Magnolia Bluff never passed. West Seattle never passed the levies. So they had to have the Central Area to bounce it off to get extra money for the school district and they were afraid that Reverend McKinney would tell his people—the church people—don’t vote for it, which he never did of course, but...

PAT: He was powerful then, and still is powerful.

BENNY: That was the difference that I remembered when I first started teaching. When we integrated they didn't ask us if we wanted to go north. First of all, after I was teaching for 6 years they sent me to Garfield. The white students were going to start going to Garfield the next year, and they said they wanted to have someone there with a friendly face that they knew. I left Garfield in January. I told the superintendent, “I didn't asked to come over here. I don't want to be here”. I was there the first semester and had no classroom and no classes. I was just walking the halls or in the library.

So they let me go back to Meany. And the next time they sent me out to work at Magnolia Bluff to work at Blaine Junior High to be in the integration that was moving, and I didn't like that at all. Schools started going downhill for me. I love teaching, but it started to go downhill I think about that time. I was doing something that I didn't ask do to, didn't wish to do. I wasn't trying to be hooked up into the Integration Movement. I was just trying to be a good teacher.

ANDREW: Were Seattle schools officially segregated?

BENNY: Not formally. It wasn't an official thing. You could go to school just about anywhere, but your chances... Well, how do you get from here to Nathan Hale on 110th? There was no transportation for you at first. So you didn't go out there. And I went out there to teach.

That was the one school I can really say I hated. When I first got there, they were telling me how this was a “country club school” because it was like 99% white. And they were not all well-off. There are some places where you had a lot of poor whites up there...

ANDREW: When you talk about Integration in Seattle you were talking about busing white kids into Garfield?

BENNY: No, you had paired schools but you didn't just bus the white kids down. When I was coaching at Nathan Hale we won the championship with an almost all-white team. And the next year, all the white kids on my team were gone because their parents set them to Blanchet—anywhere but to Garfield. You know, they sent them to private schools to keep them from ever having to go to Garfield. And I thought to myself, now I'm up here with no team because the parents didn't want them to play for a black coach. And the team the year before told me that was going to happen, but I thought if you win a championship, everybody is going to want to play for you. It didn't work that way. I just thought to myself, “My God. I didn't know that this was part of segregation.” It was difficult.

PAT: But I think a lot of that was because he was raised up here.

BENNY: Yeah, I didn't know any different.

PAT: I knew the difference the minute I got off the Trailways bus on 5th Avenue down in Seattle. On my ride up here to Seattle from Carthage, Texas, I had to ride in the backseat of the bus. I couldn't use the bus toilet. I had to hold myself until I got to the next bus depot. And sometimes at the bus depot the whites had the flushing toilets—the actual bathrooms. Black folks had to go to an out house behind the bus depot.

BENNY: I went to graduate school in Houston in ’70 and I heard them talk about it. Up here I hadn’t seen it. There were a lot of places you couldn’t go here, where they would give you the evil eye. But I went to a Greyhound bus station just in Houston to see if I could see anything that was a remnant of what they had to live through. They still had a bathroom that said “Colored Only”, and this was 1970. And then the “White Only”. And then we went downtown Carthage to that little post office and courthouse, and there's this water fountain, the one with the refrigerated cooler, that sat on the floor that said “White Only”, and then a pipe came out of the cooler to a porcelain bowl. So I drank out of the “White Only” first to see and then went over to the “Colored Only” to drink out of that. I don't know if the “Colored Only” was getting from the drain or from the water. But I just, you know, I'm going to see.

And Pat said, “Get away from there! Get away from there!”

I wasn’t trying to be brave. I just saw it on TV and I never had seen it in real life. I just tried it. Nobody said anything.

You could tell who bad it was because the presidential picture in the post office was that of Dwight Eisenhower, who had been President in the ‘50s. At that time Johnson was the President. Why didn’t they have his name up there? He's from Texas. But he just passed that Civil Rights Bill and they weren’t going for that. I just said, “My God!” It was just difficult.

Well, I didn't have little kids so the older kids didn't learn that you had to look out for someone else too. But they did look out for each other. He probably got nervous, so he had to do something to make him feel better.

TIA: Could you talk more about the formation of the Total Experience Choir and some of the trips that you guys took?

PAT: Yes. The Total Experience came about as a result of my being asked to come into the public schools to teach the black kids music. I was a member of the largest African- American church in the city and they had heard me sing and play there. I was in charge of their junior choir at the church. The lady who asked me to come and possibly work at Franklin High School was a member of the church.

So I said, “Do you want me to come and teach gospel music? In a public school?” And she said, “PAT: there's so much talent there. And I wish we had somebody who would develop it just a little bit better.”

So I went down and the principal was all for it, bless his heart. He said, “The kids sing in the hallway. They sing on the way to class. They sing out of class. They sing whenever they have free periods. They sing and it sounds really good. And I wish maybe you could help us out with that because they won’t join the organized music classes.” So I said to him, “Perhaps it's because you don't sing anything they can identify with.” And he looked at me and he said, “You might have a point there.” So I went over to Franklin and listened to their music class and said, “That would bore me to tears!”

I said, “These black kids are not going to participate in that.”

So he said, “Well, can you help us?”

And I said, “Well, my forte is African-American gospel and spirituals.”

He said, “I don't care what it is. I would just like to hear what they would sound like if they had someone to organize their talent.”

So he asked me to come in and I did. And the first thing I started playing for them, because the high school music teacher just got up and said, “Oh you play?”

And I said, “Yes.”

And so he said, “Well go ahead and play.”

The first thing I played for them was “Lift Every Voice and Sing”, the Black National Anthem. And he said, “What's that?” And I said, “America’s anthem is the Star-Spangled Banner, but African-Americans have their own as well.”

And he said, “What is it?”

And I told him it was “Lift Every Voice and Sing,” “I’ve already said that.”

So he said, ”Well let me hear how it goes.”

So I sat down, and he said, “Oh you play?”

I said, “Yeah I play.”

And he said, “Where did you get your music credentials?”

And I said, “From God.”

And he said, “What does that mean?”

“It means I've never taken a music lesson.”

And he said, “And you play?”

I said, “Yeah,” ha ha.

So he said, “Play it for me.”

So I sat down and played it for him and he said, “Well now sing it so I can hear it.” So I sang “Lift Every Voice and Sing” for him.

He said, “Where did you learn that?”

I said, "Every day in a black school in Texas and across the South, the black kids sing “Lift Every Voice and Sing.” We read scripture, we say a prayer, then the teacher said, ‘Now you are ready to learn.’ That never happens in the North. It is against every law that you have in your books. This calms the students down, regardless of what they have been through before they left home and before they got off the bus. This calms them down.”

And so he said, “Wow, we would get fired if we try that.”

And I said, “If you want me to play and organize the choir here, it's going to be gospel music.”So Mr. Wall [?], a very bright man, said to me, “Teach them what you want them to learn.”

And so I started teaching them.

“Oh Happy Day” was popular then. It was one of the first songs I taught the choir. We had so many kids at the door who refused to go to class that day, standing outside the music room door wanting to know if they could get in: “Can we come in and sit down?” “You are supposed to be in your math class. You were supposed to go to English or whatever. You can't stand out here in the hall.”

And so finally the principal said, “Just let them in.”

And I let them in. That’s when we began the Franklin High School Choir. We had 108 students to join the choir. We had no room for 108 students at that time, but somehow we managed to handle it. And that was the beginning of the Total Experience Gospel Choir right then. That's how it came into existence.

When I came to Seattle in the early ‘60s. I still had those dreams. I said at least this is farther then Carthage, Texas. Because that is a good place to just wither on the vine and die. Especially for African-Americans, because everything was totally segregated at that time, and I didn't want to just live my life in a totally segregated situation because that's no way to live. I came to Seattle and I found a lot of segregation here as well but not as blatant as it as it was in Texas.
